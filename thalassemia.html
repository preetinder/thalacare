Understanding Thalassemia

A plain-language guide for young people and families — what it is, how it's inherited, and why testing before marriage matters.

The Basics

What is Thalassemia?

Thalassemia is a genetic (inherited) blood disorder in which the body does not produce enough healthy haemoglobin — the protein inside red blood cells that carries oxygen throughout the body. When haemoglobin is deficient or abnormal, red blood cells are destroyed faster than they're produced, causing anaemia.

It is not contagious. You cannot catch Thalassemia. It is inherited — passed from parents to children through genes. Both parents must carry the faulty gene for a child to be at risk of the severe form.

Thalassemia Minor (Trait / Carrier)

The person inherits one faulty gene from one parent. They are a carrier. They typically have no symptoms, live a completely normal life, and may only have a mild, non-disabling anaemia. However, they can pass the faulty gene to their children.

Thalassemia Major

The person inherits faulty genes from both parents. This causes severe anaemia usually within the first two years of life. Children with Thalassemia Major require a blood transfusion every 2–4 weeks for life, and are at risk of organ damage, growth problems, and reduced lifespan without proper management.

Thalassemia Intermedia

A moderate form. The person may be able to maintain haemoglobin at a functional level without regular transfusions, but experiences significant symptoms. Some require transfusions during illness or pregnancy. The severity depends on the specific gene mutations involved.

Genetics & Inheritance

How is it passed on to children?

Thalassemia follows an autosomal recessive inheritance pattern. This means a child needs to inherit defective copies of the gene from both parents to develop Thalassemia Major. If only one parent is a carrier, the child may be a carrier but will not have the severe disease.

Symptoms & Diagnosis

Recognising Thalassemia

Symptoms in Thalassemia Minor

  • Usually no symptoms — person appears and feels completely healthy
  • Mild anaemia may be present — detected only by blood test
  • May be mistakenly treated as iron-deficiency anaemia (iron supplements will not help)
  • Normal life expectancy and quality of life

Symptoms in Thalassemia Major

  • Severe anaemia appearing within 6 months to 2 years of birth
  • Pale or yellowish skin, fatigue, weakness
  • Enlarged spleen and liver
  • Bone deformities, particularly in the face and skull
  • Slow growth and development in children
  • Without treatment: heart failure, organ damage, premature death
How is it Diagnosed?

Two simple blood tests

Complete Blood Count (CBC)

A routine blood test that checks haemoglobin levels and red blood cell characteristics. Low haemoglobin and smaller-than-normal red blood cells (microcytosis) may indicate carrier status. This is the first screening step.

HPLC (High Performance Liquid Chromatography)

The confirmatory test that analyses haemoglobin patterns in the blood. It accurately identifies Thalassemia Minor, Major, and other haemoglobin variants. Results are available within 24–48 hours. Available free at all our camps.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions answered

Yes, absolutely. Thalassemia Minor (carrier status) does not cause any significant health problems. Carriers live completely normal, healthy lives and have a normal life expectancy. The only concern is the possibility of passing the trait to your children if your partner is also a carrier.
There is no law in India preventing two carriers from marrying. However, couples who are both carriers are strongly advised to seek genetic counselling before starting a family. Options such as prenatal diagnosis (testing the foetus during pregnancy) and pre-implantation genetic diagnosis (during IVF) can help couples have unaffected children.
While treatment has improved significantly, Thalassemia Major remains a lifelong, life-limiting condition. Children require blood transfusions every 2–4 weeks, which leads to iron overload over time and requires additional chelation therapy. Bone marrow transplantation offers a potential cure but is expensive, risky, and requires a matched donor. Prevention through pre-marital screening is far simpler, more humane, and entirely free.
Low haemoglobin alone is not enough to confirm carrier status — it could be due to iron deficiency, Vitamin B12 deficiency, or other causes. The specific diagnostic test for Thalassemia is HPLC (High Performance Liquid Chromatography). Please visit one of our free camps for accurate testing and counselling.
India has one of the highest burdens of Thalassemia in the world. Approximately 1 in 8 Indians carries the Thalassemia trait. An estimated 10,000–12,000 babies with Thalassemia Major are born in India every year. Certain communities — including Sindhis, Punjabis, Gujaratis, Bengalis, and many others — have particularly high carrier rates.
